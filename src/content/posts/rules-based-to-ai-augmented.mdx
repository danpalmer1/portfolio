Three years of RPA at Fastenal taught me that the bottleneck of
automation is almost never the part that runs. It's the part that
decides.

A bot can fill in 200 fields a minute. It can't tell you whether the
invoice that just came in is the same vendor as the one from last
quarter who used a slightly different name. Or whether the email
attached to a ticket is the actual policy document or a draft someone
forgot to delete. Those are *judgments*, and judgments are where
rules-based automation runs out of room.

You can keep adding rules. You always can. But every rule is a
maintenance cost, and the rate at which the world produces edge cases is
higher than the rate at which one person can write rules.

## What changes with a model in the loop

Two things, and they're both quieter than I expected.

**1. The unit of work moves up a level.** Instead of writing
"if the customer name contains 'LLC', strip the suffix and re-match,"
you write "normalize this customer name to match our canonical list."
The implementation is shorter and the failure modes are more
*recognizable* — a model misreading a name fails in ways a human would
recognize, not in ways only the rule author would.

**2. The cost of the long tail collapses.** The 90% case used to be
worth automating; the 10% wasn't. With a model that handles ambiguity
gracefully, the breakeven on the long tail drops. The bot doesn't get
*better*; it gets *more general*.

## What doesn't change

A lot. Most of what made RPA work still applies — sturdy logging,
deterministic side effects, a human-readable audit trail, the discipline
of testing on data you didn't write. Models don't replace any of that.
They just shrink the part of the workflow that was *only* about
matching strings.

## The frame I'm taking into AI work

> Automate the workflow, not the UI. Automate the judgment, not the
> rule.

It's the same frame, scoped one level up. The tool is different. The
goal is the same: give people back the hours that didn't need to be
theirs.
